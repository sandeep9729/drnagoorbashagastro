COLONOSCOPY :

An examination used to detect changes or anomalies in the large intestine (colon) and rectum is a colonoscopy (koe-lun-OS-kuh-pee). A long flexible tube (colonoscope) is inserted into the rectum during a colonoscopy. At the tip of the vent, a small video camera helps the doctor to see the inside of the entire colon.

Polyps or other forms of irregular tissue may be removed during a colonoscopy via the lens, if appropriate. You can take tissue samples (biopsies) during a colonoscopy as well.

Why is that done?

A colonoscopy may be recommended by your doctor for:

Investigate the symptoms and intestinal signs :

Your doctor will be able to investigate potential causes of abdominal discomfort, rectal bleeding, persistent constipation, chronic diarrhoea, and other intestinal issues by using a colonoscopy.

Digital colon cancer screen :

If you are 50 years of age or older and have an average risk of colon cancer, your doctor may prescribe a colonoscopy every 10 years or sometimes sooner to check for colon cancer if you have no risk factors other than age. A colonoscopy is one method for screening for colon cancer. Speak about your choices with your doctor.

Check for additional polyps :

Your doctor can prescribe a follow-up colonoscopy if you have had polyps before to check for and remove any additional polyps. In order to reduce the risk of colon cancer, this is done.

Risks :

There are few complications associated with a colonoscopy. In rare cases, colonoscopy complications can include:

  • Adverse reaction during the exam to the sedative used.
  • Bleeding from the place where a sample of tissue (biopsy) was taken or a polyp or other irregular tissue was extracted.
  • A tear in the wall of the colon or rectum (perforation).

Your doctor will ask you to sign a consent form approving the operation after discussing the dangers of a colonoscopy with you.

How you prepare yourself :

You'll need to clean your (empty) colon before a colonoscopy. The view of your colon and rectum during the examination can be blurred by any residue in your colon.

The day before your test, observe a special diet :

Usually, the day before the test, you would not be able to eat solid food. Drinks may be restricted to clear beverages without milk or cream, broth, and carbonated drinks: plain water, tea and coffee. Ignore red liquids, which during a colonoscopy can be confused with blood. The night before the test, you will not be able to eat or drink anything after midnight. Usually, the doctor would prescribe that you take a laxative, either in pill form or in liquid form. In certain cases, to clear your colon, you can need to use an over-the-counter enema kit, either the night before the test or a few hours before the exam. In general, this is only useful for emptying the lower colon and is not generally recommended as a primary way to empty the colon.

Adjust your medications :

At least a week before the test, inform your doctor of your medications, particularly if you have diabetes, high blood pressure or heart issues, or if you are taking ironcontaining medications or supplements.

Even notify your doctor whether you are taking aspirin or other bloodthinning drugs, such as warfarin (Coumadin, Jantoven); new anticoagulants, such as dabigatran (Pradaxa) or rivaroxaban (Xarelto), which are used to minimise the risk of stroke or blood clots; or platelet-affecting medications, such as clopidogrel (Plavix).

You will need to change the dosages or briefly stop taking the drugs.

LIVER PROBLEMS :

The liver is an organ about the size of a football., On the right side of your belly, it lies just behind your rib cage. The liver is necessary for digesting food and ridding the body of toxic substances.

It is possible to inherit Liver Disease (genetic). A number of factors that harm the liver, such as viruses, alcohol use and obesity, can also cause liver problems.

Over time, diseases that damage the liver can lead to a lifethreatening condition called scarring (cirrhosis), which can lead to liver failure. But early treatment may allow time for the liver to heal.

Side effects :

Liver disease doesn't often cause signs and symptoms that are apparent. If liver disease signs and symptoms do occur, they can include:

  • Skin and yellowish-looking eyes (jaundice)
  • Pressure in the abdomen and swelling
  • Swelling in the ankles and legs
  • Skin itching
  • Color of black urine
  • Color of pale stool
  • Chronic exhaustion
  • Dizziness or vomiting
  • Appetite Loss
  • Tendency to bruise with ease

When to consult a physician :

If you have any recurring signs or symptoms that bother you, make an appointment with your physician. If you have stomach pain that is so serious that you can't stand still, seek urgent medical attention.

Infections :

The liver may be invaded by parasites and viruses, causing inflammation that decreases liver function. Via blood or semen, tainted food or water, or near contact with an infected person, viruses that cause liver damage may be transmitted. Hepatitis viruses include the most common forms of liver infection, including:
Hepatitis A
Hepatitis B
Hepatitis C

Abnormalism of the immune system :

Your liver may be damaged by diseases in which your immune system attacks some parts of your (autoimmune) body. Examples of liver disorders that are autoimmune include:
Hepatitis Autoimmune
Primary biliary cholangitis
Primary sclerosing cholangitis

Genetics

An irregular gene inherited from one or both of your parents will cause your liver to build up different substances, resulting in damage to the liver. Among the hereditary liver disorders are:
Hemochromatosis
Disease of Wilson
Deficiency of alpha-1 antitrypsin

Cancer and other developments

Examples are:
Cancers of the liver
Cancer in bile ducts
Adenoma of the Liver

Other

Some prevalent causes of liver disease include :
Chronic misuse of alcohol
Accumulating fat in the liver (nonalcoholic fatty liver disease)
Certain drugs for prescription or over-the-counter
Certain compounds of herbs

Factors of Risk

The following are factors that can raise your risk of liver disease:
Usage of strong alcohol
Adiposity
Diabetes Type 2
Tattoos or piercings for the body
Using mutual needles to inject drugs
Transfusion of Blood Before 1992
Exposure to blood and body fluids from others
Sex that is unprotected
Exposure to certain chemicals or toxins
Family history of a condition of the liver

Prevention

To stop liver disease:

Drink alcohol in moderation :

That means up to one drink a day for females for healthy adults and up to two drinks a day for males. More than eight drinks a week for women and more than 15 drinks a week for men are described as heavy or high-risk drinking.

Just get vaccinated :

Speak to your doctor about getting hepatitis A and hepatitis B vaccines if you are at elevated risk of contracting hepatitis or if you have already been infected with some sort of hepatitis virus.

Using drugs carefully :

Taking prescription and non-prescription medications only when required and in prescribed doses only. Don't combine alcohol and drugs. Before combining herbal supplements or prescription or nonprescription medications, consult with your doctor.

Avoid contact with the blood and bodily fluids of other individuals :

Accidental needle sticks or improper cleaning of blood or body fluids may spread hepatitis viruses.

Keep food safe for you :

Before eating or cooking food, wash your hands thoroughly. Using bottled water to drink, wash your hands and brush your teeth while travelling in a developing world.

For aerosol sprays, take note :

In a well-ventilated environment, be sure to use these items and wear a mask when spraying insecticides, fungicides, paint and other toxic chemicals. Often follow orders from the manufacturer.

Have your skin covered :

Wear gloves, long sleeves, a hat and a mask when using insecticides and other toxic substances, so that chemicals are not absorbed into your skin.

Maintain a balanced weight :

Obesity can cause disease of the nonalcoholic fatty liver.
