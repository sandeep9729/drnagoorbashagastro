GASTRIC BANDING

A medical treatment for obesity is gastric banding. Such forms of intervention are referred to as bariatric surgery. The procedure constricts the stomach, so that after eating less food than normal, a person feels full.
Fast facts on gastric band surgery
Here are some key points about surgery on the gastric band.

  • A type of weight loss, or bariatric, surgery is gastric band surgery.
  • To reduce food intake, it is a minimally invasive, quick, reversible and flexible surgical procedure that reduces the size of the stomach.
  • It involves putting an inflatable band around the top of the stomach, resulting in a person with less food feeling fuller.
  • After surgery, a strict liquid-only diet with the gradual reintroduction of soft foods must be observed for up to 6 weeks.
  • Banding can lead to considerable weight loss and diabetes, but weight loss varies considerably.

How does it work.?

Gastric banding is a form of surgery for weight loss that involves putting a silicone band around the upper part of the stomach to minimise the size of the stomach and decrease the intake of food.
It is approved by the Food and Drug Administration for use as a weight loss medication (FDA).

The surgeon puts the band around the stomach's upper portion and adds a tube to the band. Under the skin of the abdomen, the tube is accessible via a port.
The surgeon injects saline solution into the band through this port to inflate it.

The amount of constriction around the stomach may be altered by changes. A small stomach pouch above it is formed by the band, with the rest of the stomach below.

The amount of food that the stomach will carry at any time is decreased by having a smaller stomach pouch. The effect is an increased feeling of fullness after consuming a smaller amount of food. In exchange, this decreases appetite and helps lower the total consumption of food.

An benefit of this type of bariatric procedure is that without malabsorption, it helps the body to digest as normal.

SURGERY

Under general anaesthesia, a gastric band is mounted. In an outpatient clinic, it is normally done, and the person usually goes later on the same day.

The process is minimally invasive. It is carried out by incisions in the keyhole. In the abdomen, between one and five minor surgical cuts are made by the surgeon. Using a laparoscope, a long narrow tube with a monitor, surgery is carried out. Sometimes the operation takes 30 to 60 minutes.

From midnight before the day of the procedure, the patient does not feed. In 2 days, most individuals may resume most daily activities, but they will need to take a week's leave from work.

DIET AFTER SURGERY :

The consumption of food must be reduced initially.

  • The diet is limited to water and fluids for the first few days, such as thin soups.
  • Liquids and blended foods such as yoghurt and puréed vegetables can be consumed until the end of 4 weeks.
  • Soft foods are introduced from 4 to 6 weeks.
  • The person will resume a regular diet after 6 weeks.

BENEFITS :

Laparoscopic gastric banding's benefits include:

  • The likelihood of weight loss for those with obesity in the long term
  • Relatively fast recovery
  • Less risk of wound infections and hernias following surgery
  • Decreased risk of diabetes, hypertension, urinary incontinence and other disorders correlated with excess weight
  • No lack of absorption of nutrients
  • In certain cases, changes in the quality of life after surgery

POLYPECTOMY

A polypectomy is a procedure, also called the large intestine, used to extract polyps from the inside of the colon. An irregular array of tissue is a polyp. The operation is generally non-invasive and is usually done with a colonoscopy at the same time.

PURPOSE OF POLYPECTOMY

Many colon tumours develop as a benign (noncancerous)
development until they become malignant (cancerous).

In order to detect the presence of any polyps, a colonoscopy is first performed. If any are detected, a polypectomy is performed and the tissue is removed. In order to decide whether the growths are cancerous, precancerous, or benign, the tissue will be tested. Colon cancer may prevent this.

Polyps are also not at all associated with any symptoms. Larger polyps, however can cause:

  • Rectal haemorrhage
  • Pressure in the abdomen
  • Intestinal abnormalities
A polypectomy can also assist in relieving these symptoms. This procedure is required at any time during a colonoscopy when polyps are found.

PROCEDURE

Usually, a polypectomy is performed at the same time as a colonoscopy. During a colonoscopy, a colonoscope may be inserted into your rectum so your doctor can see all segments of your colon. A colonoscope with a camera and a light at the end of it is a long thin, flexible tube.

For people over 50 years of age, a colonoscopy is regularly given to search for any development that could be suggestive of cancer. During your colonoscopy, if your doctor discovers polyps, they will usually perform a polypectomy at the same time.

There are many ways in which it is possible to execute a polypectomy. It will depend on what kind of polyps are in the colon what direction the doctor prefers.

Polyps may be tiny, huge, pedunculated, or sessile. Sessile polyps are flat and have no stalks at all. Pedunculated polyps develop like mushrooms on stalks. Biopsy forceps may be used for removal in the case of small polyps (less than 5 millimetres in diameter). Using a snare, larger polyps may be removed (up to 2 centimetres in diameter).

Your doctor will loop a thin wire around the bottom of the polyp during snare polypectomy and use heat to cut off the growth. Any tissue or stalk that remains is then cauterised.

How long is it going to take to recover?

Following a polypectomy, you can not drive for 24 hours.
Recovery is normally rapid. Minor side effects typically resolve within 24 hours, such as gas, bloating, and cramps. A complete recovery will take up to two weeks with a more involved procedure.
Your doctor will provide you with some guidance for how to take care of yourself. For two or three days after the operation, they can ask you to avoid some beverages and foods that may irritate your digestive system.

  • tea
  • coffee
  • soda
  • alcohol
  • Spicy foods
Your doctor will also schedule you for a follow-up colonoscopy. It is important to verify that the polypectomy has been successful and that no new polyps have formed.
What risks and side effects are there?
The hazards of a polypectomy can include intestinal perforation or rectal bleeding. For a colonoscopy, these threats are similar. Complications are uncommon, but if you have any of the following symptoms, contact your physician immediately:
  • fever or chills, as these could indicate an infection
    • heavy bleeding severe pain or bloating in your abdomen vomiting irregular heartbeat

METAL STENTS

The initial treatment choice for pancreatic fluid collections is called endoscopic transmural drainage. In addition to emerging data on bile duct and gallbladder drainage, lumen-apposing metal stents have recently been developed for drainage of pancreatic fluid collections under endoscopic ultrasound guidance.

With the reporting of pooled data analysis on technological performance, clinical success and complication rate of endoscopic ultrasound-guided lumen-apposing metal stents, we aimed to conduct a systematic examination. A MEDLINE/PubMed and EMBASE search was performed for all lumen-apposing metal stent studies used for pancreatic, biliary and other indications. Security, problems, and yield data have been extracted and used in the final pooled analysis.

Overall, 19 articles dealing with pancreatic fluid collections drainage, 18 articles reporting on bile duct drainage and 19 articles related to gallbladder drainage were reported. In all disease states, technological and clinical achievements were high and achieved more than 95 percent . The incidence of complications in the accumulation of pancreatic fluid, bile duct and gallbladder drainage was 12%, 11.2% and 9.8%, respectively. Endoscopic lumen-guided ultrasoundapposing metal stents are technically feasible with very high success rate and acceptable complications rate.
