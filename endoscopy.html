Endoscopy

Endoscopy is the penetration directly into the body of a long, thin tube to observe in detail an inner organ or tissue. Other functions, including imaging and minor surgery, may also be done using it. Endoscopes are minimally invasive and for example, the mouth or anus may be inserted into the openings of the body. Alternatively, for instance, in the knee or belly, they may be inserted through small incisions. Performed through a small incision and aided by special instruments, including the endoscope, is called keyhole surgery. Because modern endoscopy has relatively few complications, offers accurate pictures, and is easy to conduct, it has proven to be extremely useful in many medical fields. Tens of millions of endoscopies are conducted annually today.

Fast facts on endoscopy

Here are some key points about endoscopy. More detail and supporting information is in the main article.

  • Endoscopies are procedures that are quick and reasonably safe.
  • It was in 1806 that the first endoscope was made.
  • Investigation, clarification, and care are the key reasons for the endoscopy.
  • Tumors or polyps from the digestive tract may be removed by endoscopy.

TYPES

Endoscopy is useful for the analysis of many human body systems; these fields include:

  • Gastrointestinal tract: oesophagus, stomach and duodenum (esophagogastroduodenoscopy), large intestine/colon (colonoscopy, sigmoidoscopy), small intestine (enteroscopy), bile duct, rectum (rectoscopy), and anus (anoscopy).
  • Respiratory tract: nose, lower respiratory tract (rhinoscopy) (bronchoscopy).
  • The Ear: Otoscopy
  • Urinary tract: Cystoscopy
  • Gynoscopy of the female reproductive tract: cervix (colposcopy), womb (hysteroscopy), fallopian tubes (falloposcopy).
  • Small incisions: the abdominal or pelvic cavity (laparoscopy), the inside of the joint (arthroscopy), the chest organs (thoracoscopy and mediastinoscopy).

PREPARATION

The procedure does not require a hospital overnight stay and usually only takes about 1 hour to complete. Instructions on the planning of the operation will be given by the doctor.

The person needs to fast for about 12 hours for several forms of endoscopy, though this varies depending on the type.

For procedures examining the stomach, laxatives may be taken to clear the system the night before.

Before the endoscopy, a doctor will perform an examination. All current drugs (including supplements) and any previous procedures should be listed.

PROCEDURE

To a degree, the treatment will depend on the reason for the endoscopy.

There are three main reasons why an endoscopy is performed:

Research :

For example if a person has vomiting, abdominal pain, breathing problems, stomach ulcers, trouble swallowing, or gastrointestinal bleeding, an endoscope can be used to check for a trigger.

Diagnostic confirmation :

Endoscopy can be used to perform a biopsy to confirm that cancer or other diseases have been identified.

Treatment :

An endoscope can be used directly to treat a disease; for instance, endoscopy can be used to cauterise a bleeding vessel or dissolve a polyp (seal using heat).

Endoscopy is often paired with another technique, such as an ultrasonic scan. It can be used to position the ultrasound probe close to organs that like the pancreas, can be difficult to visualise.

Responsive lights that use narrow band imaging are often fitted with modern endoscopes. This form of imaging uses unique wavelengths of blue and green that make it easier for the doctor to detect precancerous conditions.

Usually, an endoscopy is done when the patient is awake, although the patient may often receive local anaesthetic (usually an anaesthetic spray at the back of the throat); the patient is also sedated.

A mouth guard may be used to secure the teeth and lips when the tube is inserted for endoscopy procedures requiring entrance through the mouth.

Endoscope use in surgery :

In recent years, endoscopy has improved to allow certain types of surgery to be done using a modified endoscope; this makes the surgery less invasive.

Procedures such as gallbladder removal, sealing and binding of the fallopian tubes, and removal from the digestive system or lungs of small tumours are now commonplace.

The adapted endoscope used for keyhole surgery is a laparoscope (also referred to as laparoscopic surgery).

Just a small incision is needed for laparoscopic surgery and can be used for appendectomy (removal of the appendix), hysterectomy (removal of the uterus), and prostatectomy (removal of prostate tissue).

Using this process, during and after surgery, patients lose less blood and can heal much faster compared with traditional surgical procedures.
