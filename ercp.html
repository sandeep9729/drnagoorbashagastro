ERCP

A technique used to diagnose diseases of the gallbladder, biliary system, pancreas, and liver is ERCP (short for endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography). The test looks "upstream" from where digestive fluid comes to where it reaches the intestinesâ€”the liver, gallbladder, and pancreas. Furthermore, ERCP can be used in the areas of digestive system to treat problems.

During ERCP, what happens?

A gastroenterologist (a physician specialising in the treatment of gastrointestinal diseases) uses a special endoscope (a long flexible tube with a light and a camera at the end) to examine the inside of the digestive system throughout ERCP. The doctor decides where the bile duct reaches the intestine and then feeds into the duct a thin catheter (a plastic tube) and squirts in a contrast agent when taking X-rays.
The doctor may then treat it by performing one of the following procedures once the source of the problem is identified.

Sphincterotomy

This includes making a small incision (cut) in the opening of the pancreatic duct or bile duct, which will allow the proper drainage of small gallstones, bile, and pancreatic juice.

Placement Stent.

A stent is a drainage tube that is inserted in the bile duct or the pancreatic duct to keep the duct open and allow it to drain.

Removal of Gallstone (s).

ERCP, but not from the gallbladder itself can remove gallstones from the bile duct.

Is the ERCP secure?

An ERCP is considered a low-risk operation; complications may occur, however. This may include pancreatitis, bowel perforation, infections, and bleeding. Patients undergoing ERCP for treatment, for example for the removal of gallstones, face a higher risk of complications than patients undergoing diagnostic testing. Before the procedure, the physician will address the implications of potential complications.

How Can I Get ERCP Prepared?

Inform your doctor when you have ERCP of any special medical conditions you have including:

  • For pregnancy.
  • Lung situations.
  • Cardiac situations.
  • Allergies to any substance.

Using insulin if you have diabetes. On the day of the exam, you can need to change the dose of insulin. Through this change, the diabetes care provider will help you.
Before the operation, do not eat or drink anything for eight hours.

During sedation, you may feel drowsy for some time, so you can ask for assistance from a responsible adult who can drive you home after the treatment. For at least eight hours, you should not drive or operate machinery because the medication provided during the procedure may cause drowsiness.

After the operation, you will need to stay in the hospital overnight, so pack personal things that you may need.

Warning About ERCP
