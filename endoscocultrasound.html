ENDOSCOPIC ULTRSOUND

In order to evaluate digestive (gastrointestinal) and lung conditions, endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) is a minimally invasive technique. To create clear images of the lining and walls of your digestive tract and chest, nearby organs such as the pancreas and liver, and lymph nodes, a special endoscope utilises high-frequency sound waves.

EUS helps the doctor to sample (biopsy) fluid and tissue from your belly or chest for examination when combined with a technique called fine-needle aspiration. A minimally invasive alternative to exploratory surgery may be EUS with fine-needle aspiration.

In such procedures, such as draining pseudocysts, EUS methods are also used.

Why is that done?

EUS is used to classify the cause of symptoms such as pain in the abdomen or chest, to ascertain the nature of diseases in the digestive tract and lungs, and to measure the effects of imaging tests such as CT or MRI scans.

The EUS can help in the assessment of:

  • Cancer of the colon, oesophagus, liver, pancreas or stomach, as well as cancer of the ampulla and rectum
  • lymphoma
  • Esophagus Barrett's
  • Neuroendocrine tumors
  • Pancreatitis and pancreatic cysts
  • Bile duct stones
  • Sarcoidosis

EUS can help:

  • In esophageal, gastric, rectal, pancreatic and lung tumours, determine how deeply a tumour penetrates the abdominal wall
  • If present, determine the magnitude (stage) of cancer
  • Determine if cancer has spread to the lymph nodes or other organs (metastasized).
  • Providing correct details to guide care for non-small cell lung cancer cells
  • Assess abnormal findings from imaging scans, such as pancreatic cysts
  • Drainage guide for pseudocysts and other irregular fluid collections in the abdomen
  • Permit precise targeting directly into the pancreas, liver and other organs for delivery of medication

EUS is conducted on an outpatient basis and is well-tolerated by most individuals.

If you have had abdominal surgery that has altered your anatomy, such as the Roux-en-Y gastric bypass, you might not be an EUS candidate.

Risks :

In general, EUS is secure when done at a centre with an experienced and competent health care staff. Your doctor will discuss the possibility of EUS complications with you. Fine-needle aspiration is most often associated with these threats.

Risks can involve:

Hemorrhage
Infections
Intestinal wall or throat tearing (perforation)
Pancreatitis, if the pancreas is fine-needle-aspirated

You can reduce your risk of complications by following the advice of your doctor to plan for the EUS carefully.

If you experience any of the following signs or symptoms, call your doctor immediately or go to an emergency room

  • A fever
  • Pain in the chest
  • Shortness of Respiration
  • Stool, black or rather dark-colored
  • Abdominal pain that is serious or chronic

How you ready yourself

Your doctor will give you specific instructions to prepare for your EUS. You may be asked to:

Fast until EUS :

And make sure that your belly is empty. Generally, for at least 6 hours before the exam, you'll be asked to fast.

When EUS is performed in the rectal region, prepare your rectum.

Before the treatment, you may be asked to take a laxative or have an enema and to adopt a liquid diet.

Avoid taking such drugs, such as blood thinners,

for example. When fine-needle aspiration is performed during the EUS, blood thinners can increase your risk of bleeding. Your doctor will give you detailed guidance on your drugs if you have chronic illnesses, such as diabetes or high blood pressure.

If you are sedated before the EUS,

prepare ahead for your recovery. To calm them, most people who have EUS are given medicine. After the treatment, arrange for someone to bring you home.

Results

The EUS images will be interpreted by a specialist in digestive diseases (gastroenterologist) or lung disease (pulmonologist) with special EUS training. If you have fine-needle aspiration, a doctor qualified in testing biopsies (pathologist) will report the test results.
